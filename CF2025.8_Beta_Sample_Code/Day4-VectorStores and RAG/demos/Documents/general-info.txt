General Knowledge Base — World Facts and Trivia

The Solar System
Our solar system consists of eight planets orbiting the Sun. Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun, followed by Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Earth is the only known planet to support life. The Sun accounts for approximately 99.86% of the total mass of the solar system. Jupiter is the largest planet with a diameter of about 139,820 kilometers. Mars is often called the Red Planet due to iron oxide on its surface. Saturn is famous for its prominent ring system made of ice and rock particles.

World Geography
The Earth has seven continents: Africa, Antarctica, Asia, Australia, Europe, North America, and South America. The Pacific Ocean is the largest and deepest ocean, covering more than 60 million square miles. Mount Everest, located in the Himalayas, is the tallest mountain above sea level at 8,849 meters. The Amazon River is the largest river by discharge volume of water. The Sahara Desert is the largest hot desert in the world, covering much of North Africa. Lake Baikal in Russia is the deepest freshwater lake in the world.

History of Computing
The first electronic general-purpose computer, ENIAC, was built in 1945. It weighed about 30 tons and occupied 1,800 square feet. The transistor was invented in 1947 at Bell Labs. The first programming language, Fortran, was developed by IBM in 1957. The internet originated from ARPANET, a project funded by the US Department of Defense in the late 1960s. Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web in 1989 while working at CERN. The first smartphone, IBM Simon, was released in 1994.

Cooking Basics
Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level. Salt enhances flavor in most dishes. Olive oil has a smoke point of about 190 degrees Celsius. Baking requires precise measurements, while cooking allows more flexibility. Yeast is a living organism that makes bread rise through fermentation. Chocolate was first consumed as a bitter beverage by the ancient Mayans and Aztecs.

Music Theory
Music is organized into scales, with the most common being major and minor scales. An octave contains 12 semitones in Western music. The tempo of a piece determines how fast or slow it is played. Beethoven composed his Ninth Symphony while almost completely deaf. The guitar has six strings in standard tuning. A chord is three or more notes played simultaneously.
